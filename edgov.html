Usability Questions

  • Without looking at the homepage, what do you expect to find on the site?

  • What kind of information do you think you’ll find on each page? (Ask for each tab)

  • Imagine you’re a current grad student seeking to repay your loans from college. How would you go about accomplishing this?

  • If you are also looking to take out additional loans and grants to pay for grad school, what would you do on this site to accomplish that?

Final Thoughts and Future Features

Designing websites that are accessible and maintain a cohesive style can help put users at ease and create a more enjoyable experience, especially when users visit government websites that tend to be extremely information dense.
Having more time to conduct further usability testing and research would be useful to guide our future developments & ensure we are not losing focus because of our own biases.

In the future we would consider making additional changes such as:

  • Removing tertiary navigation in the mobile version (some users struggled with it and felt it was confusing)
  • Reorder categories in top bar navigation to promote more intuitive navigation
  • Adding even more contextual information and definitions on pages such as “Eligibility Requirements” to make the website easier to digest for users who may be unfamiliar with certain government terms.
